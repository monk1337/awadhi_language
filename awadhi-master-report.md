# PART 1: THE HOOK — WHY ANYONE SHOULD CARE

## The Single Most Devastating Comparison

India's 2011 Census recorded **3,850,906** Awadhi speakers. Independent linguistic estimates place the actual number between **38 and 65 million** — a gap of tenfold to seventeenfold. Politicians in Uttar Pradesh have claimed the number is closer to **80 million**. This makes Awadhi one of the largest language undercounts in human history.

The reason is bureaucratic: since 1961, the Indian Census has classified Awadhi not as a language but as a **"mother tongue under Hindi,"** so most speakers simply check the "Hindi" box. The language has been statistically erased while its speakers remain very much alive.

**The most defensible estimate: ~38 million in India + ~864,000 in Nepal + ~650,000+ in diaspora = approximately 39–40 million globally.** The census figure of 3.85 million is universally acknowledged — even by India's own Linguistic Survey (2023) — as a severe undercount.

### The European comparison that should shock

Put 38 million in context:

| Language | Speakers | Official status |
|----------|----------|-----------------|
| Danish | ~6.0 million | Official language of Denmark, EU recognized |
| Norwegian | ~5.0 million | National language of Norway (two written standards) |
| Finnish | ~5.4 million | National language of Finland |
| Estonian | ~1.1 million | Official language of Estonia |
| Icelandic | ~350,000 | Official language of Iceland |
| Welsh | ~880,000 | Protected, government-funded, annual surveys |
| Basque | ~750,000 | Co-official in Spain, protected by law |
| **All seven combined** | **~19.5 million** | **All fully recognized and funded** |
| **Awadhi** | **~38 million** | **Zero recognition, zero funding, zero schools** |

Awadhi has more speakers than Danish, Norwegian, Finnish, Estonian, Icelandic, Welsh, and Basque **combined** — yet every one of those languages enjoys full government recognition, institutional infrastructure, and constitutional protection. Awadhi has none.

Sanskrit has 24,821 speakers and is constitutionally recognized. Awadhi has approximately 38 million speakers and is not. This is not a matter of opinion. These are the government's own numbers.

### The cultural weight vs. official status gap

- The **Ramcharitmanas**, written in Awadhi by the poet-saint Tulsidas in 1574–75, has had **108.7 million copies printed by one press alone**. It is present in virtually every Hindu household across North India. Mahatma Gandhi called it **"the greatest book of all devotional literature."** Historian Vincent Smith called Tulsidas "the greatest man of his age in India, greater even than Akbar himself."
- In **May 2024**, UNESCO inscribed Ramcharitmanas manuscripts in its **Memory of the World Asia-Pacific Register**. UNESCO simultaneously recognizes the TEXT but India doesn't recognize the LANGUAGE.
- The **Hanuman Chalisa** — the most widely recited Hindu prayer on Earth, chanted daily by hundreds of millions — is composed in Awadhi. Most devotees have no idea they are reciting lines in a language their own government classifies as a mere dialect.
- **Ramlila** — the dramatic tradition of performing the Ramayana story — is recognized by UNESCO as **Intangible Cultural Heritage**. It is performed in Awadhi. The language it's performed in has no official status.
- According to Lexvo (2001 data), Awadhi ranks **29th in the world** by number of native speakers — yet it is absent from most "top language" infographics and lists.

# PART 2: ORIGIN STORY — FROM SACRED PLAINS TO "DIALECT" CHECKBOX

## Awadhi and Hindi have different ancestors

The claim that Awadhi is a "dialect of Hindi" collapses the moment you trace the family tree. The foundational academic study establishing Awadhi's genealogy is **Baburam Saksena's *Evolution of Awadhi: A Branch of Hindi* (1938, Indian Press, Allahabad)**.

- **Standard Hindi** descends from **Sauraseni Prakrit** through Sauraseni Apabhramsha — the speech of the western Gangetic plain around Delhi.
- **Awadhi** descends from a different ancestor entirely: **Ardhamagadhi Prakrit** — the eastern literary Prakrit that was also the sacred language of Jain scripture.

From Ardhamagadhi Apabhramsha, three siblings emerged as the "Eastern Hindi" group — **Awadhi, Bagheli, and Chhattisgarhi** — each distinct from the Western Hindi group (Khari Boli, Braj Bhasha, Haryanvi) that Standard Hindi would eventually be drawn from.

**Calling Awadhi a "dialect of Hindi" is roughly equivalent to calling Portuguese a "dialect of Spanish" — they share a family, but they diverged from different parents centuries ago.**

## The timeline

### Phase 1: Emergence (6th–13th century CE)
- Ardhamagadhi Prakrit gives way to eastern Apabhramsha languages
- The speech of the region around Ayodhya (ancient Kosala, Rama's kingdom) begins to crystallize into something recognizably "Awadhi"
- The earliest confirmed Awadhi writing dates to the **12th century**, attributed to **Damodara Pandita**
- The very name "Awadhi" links to Awadh, the political-cultural region, and further back to ancient Kosala

### Phase 2: The Golden Age (1379–1623 CE)
- Awadhi was **the prestige literary language of North India** — not a village dialect
- It surpassed Sanskrit in popular reach and challenged Persian's court monopoly
- Two extraordinary literary streams converged:

**The Sufi premakhyan tradition:**
Eastern Sufis, particularly from the Chishti order, deliberately chose Awadhi to reach common people, rejecting the elitism of Arabic, Persian, and Sanskrit. They took the Persian *masnavi* poetic form and filled it with Indian stories, Hindu mythology, and yogic imagery.

- **Maulana Da'ud, *Chandayan* (1379 CE)** — the first major literary work in recognizable Awadhi, a Sufi romance
- **Qutban, *Mirigavati* (c. 1503)** — continued the premakhyan tradition
- **Malik Muhammad Jayasi, *Padmavat* (1540)** — a monumental Sufi allegory; a Muslim mystic writes a Hindu Rajput romance in Awadhi
- **Manjhan, *Madhumalati* (1545)** — another major premakhyan

These were Muslim poets writing Hindu love stories in a Hindu vernacular, using Sufi philosophy as the allegorical frame. The cross-cultural synthesis was unprecedented.

**The Bhakti devotional movement:**
Awadhi became the language of Rama devotion (just as Braj Bhasha became the language of Krishna devotion).

- **Kabir (c.1398–1518)** — mystic poet whose *Bijak* was classified by George Grierson as "written in old form of Awadhi language" (Mirzapuri dialect). 541 of his hymns were included in the Sikh *Guru Granth Sahib*, making Awadhi words part of Sikh sacred literature.
- **Nath yogi Gorakhnath** — wrote verses in Awadhi
- **Goswami Tulsidas (1532–1623)** — composed the *Ramcharitmanas* (1574–75), deliberately choosing the people's language over elite Sanskrit. The Brahmin establishment "tried to humiliate Tulsidas while also making several attempts to kill him" for breaking Sanskrit's monopoly on sacred literature.

**Narrative for the website: "There was a time when if you wanted to move hearts in North India, you reached for Awadhi."**

### Phase 3: The Nawabi era (17th–19th century)
- Under the patronage of the **Nawabs of Awadh**, Awadhi absorbed Persian and Arabic influences
- Developed exquisite courtly refinement governing poetry, administration, and culinary arts
- Awadhi was the language of the people — the tongue of markets, homes, and folk theater — even as Persian and Urdu dominated royal courts
- Its gentle, polite cadence ("tehzeeb") shaped Lucknow's famed culture of courtesy

### Phase 4: The Colonial Erasure (19th century)

Three decisive blows killed Awadhi's trajectory:

**1. Fort William College (1800):**
Established in Calcutta by Lord Wellesley to train British civil servants, it became "the birthplace of modern Hindi" (Dutch literary historian Thomas De Bruijn). The Hindi taught there was based on **Khari Boli**, the dialect of the Delhi region — chosen not for literary richness but for **administrative convenience**, since it was spoken near the colonial power center. Professor **Lallu Lal** produced prose works in Sanskritized Khari Boli, creating a standardized written language where none had existed.

Before this, as Christopher R. King documented in *One Language, Two Scripts* (OUP, 1994): **"Other languages such as Awadhi, Braj Bhasha, and Sadhukaddi were preferred by littérateurs."** Khari Boli was considered rough and unsophisticated — the term literally means "standing/stiff language," contrasted with the "mellifluousness" of Awadhi.

*Had Fort William College chosen Awadhi as the basis for standardized Hindi instead of Khari Boli, the linguistic identity of 500+ million people would be fundamentally different today. History turned on an administrative convenience.*

**2. The 1837 language switch:**
The British East India Company abolished Persian as the language of the lower administrative courts in the North-Western Provinces. Rather than replacing it with the widely spoken Awadhi, the administration adopted Urdu in Persian script, and later actively standardized Sanskritized Khari Boli.

**3. The 1857 suppression:**
After the 1857 War of Independence — in which Awadh was a **hotspot of rebellion** — British repression "effectively silenced" Awadhi from public life.

**4. The script wars (1893):**
The **Nagari Pracharini Sabha**, founded in 1893, aggressively lobbied for Devanagari script over both Persian script and Kaithi script. In **1854, there were 77,368 school primers in Kaithi versus only 25,151 in Devanagari** — Kaithi had 3× more textbooks. But this campaign succeeded in marginalizing both Awadhi's literary tradition and the neutral script it had used.

### Phase 5: Post-Independence Erasure (1950–present)

- **1950:** Hindi (meaning Khari Boli in Devanagari) declared India's official language under Article 343. Awadhi classified as a "mother tongue under Hindi."
- **1971 Census:** The grouping was expanded from **10 to 48 languages** under the Hindi umbrella, inflating Hindi's official speaker count from **30.39% to 43.63%** of the population overnight. Awadhi, Bhojpuri, Rajasthani, Chhattisgarhi, and dozens of other distinct languages were bureaucratically devoured.
- **To this day:** Awadhi has no Eighth Schedule recognition, no official status at any level of Indian government, and is not formally taught in any Indian institution.

**The emotional story: a language that once carried kings and saints has been demoted to something rural, informal, and invisible — without ever actually disappearing.**

# PART 3: THE MAP — WHERE AWADHI IS ALIVE

## The heartland: ~23 districts across Uttar Pradesh

Awadhi's core territory is the historic Awadh region of central-eastern Uttar Pradesh, the land that takes its name from the city of Ayodhya. The language is spoken across approximately **23 core districts**, bounded by Kannauji and Bundeli dialects to the west, Bhojpuri to the east, Bagheli to the south, and the Nepal border to the north.

**Northern and central districts:** Lucknow, Barabanki, Rae Bareli, Amethi, Unnao, Fatehpur, Sitapur, Lakhimpur Kheri, Bahraich, Shrawasti

**Eastern districts (transitioning toward Bhojpuri):** Ayodhya (formerly Faizabad), Sultanpur, Pratapgarh, Ambedkar Nagar, Gonda, Balrampur, Prayagraj (Allahabad), Kaushambi, Bhadohi, Mirzapur, Varanasi

**Partial coverage:** Western portions of Basti, Siddharthnagar, Jaunpur

**Fringe areas:** Parts of northern Madhya Pradesh (where closely related Bagheli dominates), migration-destination cities like Delhi and Mumbai

## Nepal: 8 districts and growing recognition

Awadhi is spoken in **eight districts of Nepal's Terai region**:

- **Lumbini Zone:** Kapilvastu, Rupandehi, Nawalparasi
- **Bheri Zone:** Banke, Bardiya
- **Rapti Zone:** Dang
- **Far west:** Kailali, Kanchanpur

The urban center of Awadhi life in Nepal is **Nepalgunj**.

Nepal's census trajectory shows remarkable growth:
- 1991: 374,638 (2.03%)
- 2011: 501,752 (1.89%)
- **2021: 864,276 (2.96%) — a 72% increase in one decade**

Nepal's Language Commission has **recommended Awadhi as an official language of Lumbini Province** — the province that contains the birthplace of the Buddha.

**Key contrast: While Awadhi shrinks in India, it's growing 72% in Nepal. A foreign country is moving faster than India to recognize a language born on Indian soil.**

## The Girmitiya journey: from Ayodhya to the South Pacific

Between 1834 and 1920, the British Empire's **Indian indenture system** transported over **1.2 million** Indians to colonial plantations after the abolition of slavery. The laborers called themselves **Girmitiya** (their pronunciation of "agreement," the indenture contract they signed) and **Jahajis** ("ship people").

The majority came from precisely the Awadh and Bhojpur regions of eastern UP and Bihar. Ships departed from Calcutta carrying people from Basti, Gonda, Lucknow, Faizabad, Sultanpur, and Gorakhpur — the same districts that are Awadhi's heartland today.

### Fiji Hindi: Awadhi's greatest survival story

Linguistic analysis shows that **34.5% of indentured laborers in Fiji were Awadhi speakers** — the single largest linguistic group — with 33.4% Bhojpuri speakers. The koiné that emerged is classified by Ethnologue as "a type of Awadhi influenced by Bhojpuri."

Specific Awadhi features that survive in Fiji Hindi:
- Imperfective suffixes *-taa/-at*
- Third-person perfective suffixes *-is* and *-in*
- Genitive pronouns **mor** (my) and **tor** (your)
- The number **dui** (two) instead of Standard Hindi's *do*
- Phonological: /ʃ/ → /s/ (so *shaadi* → *saadi*), and /ʋ/ → /b/

Fiji Hindi absorbed local vocabulary: Fijian words for flora and fauna (*kawakawa, sambutu, kanade, damu* — fish types), colonial English (*kantap* from "cane top," *laibāri* from "library," *aspatal* from "hospital").

Fiji Hindi has been an **official language of Fiji since 1997** — recognized by its constitution, used in parliament, taught in universities. The University of the South Pacific offers courses. A Bible translation was completed in 2002. Films are produced in it (*Adhura Sapna*, 2007; *Ghar Pardes*; *Langha Phobia*). The language has been transmitted through **six generations**.

**The irony bears repeating: Awadhi-derived Fiji Hindi is an official language recognized by Fiji's parliament, constitution, and courts. The language it descends from has no official recognition in India.**

### Caribbean and Indian Ocean

**Suriname:** Awadhi blended with Bhojpuri to form **Sarnami Hindustani** — ~250,000 speakers in Suriname and the Netherlands. The **Baithak Gana** tradition — neo-traditional seated singing — preserves folk melodies rooted in Awadhi and Bhojpuri. **Chutney music**, blending Caribbean soca with Indian folk roots, carries Awadhi DNA into contemporary Caribbean pop culture.

**Trinidad & Tobago and Guyana:** Trinidadian Hindustani (locally called *Gaon ke Bolee*, "village speech") is severely endangered. By the third generation (mid-20th century), English had largely displaced Indian languages. Sundar Popo's Chutney music popularized Trinidadian Hindustani-English fusion. **Caribbean Hindustani Inc.**, led by **Visham Bhimull**, works across all three countries on preservation.

**Mauritius:** Bhojpuri-Awadhi crashed from **31.7% of the population using it as home language in 1972** to just **5.1% in 2022**. Mauritian Creole has largely displaced it.

**South Africa:** The shift to English was complete by mid-20th century. Today it is "rare to find a home in which Hindi is spoken daily." Only the Ramcharitmanas — carried by immigrants in 1860 — keeps Awadhi alive in religious recitation.

# PART 4: WHAT AWADHI SOUNDS LIKE

## The acoustic signature

To an untrained ear accustomed to Delhi Hindi, Awadhi sounds softer, more musical, more "sung." The differences are immediate:

### Key sound shifts

- **/ʃ/ → /s/**: "shaadi" (wedding) becomes **"saadi"**
- **/ʋ/ → /b/**: "videsh" (foreign land) becomes **"bidesh"**
- **/pʰ/ and /f/ merge**: Aspiration neutralized, giving a relaxed earthiness
- **Consonant clusters softened**: Hindi's *karma* becomes Awadhi's **karam** — vowels inserted, creating softer rhythm
- **Voiceless (whispered) vowels**: Certain vowels are pronounced with a breathy, ghostly quality, giving Awadhi an unusually musical, lilting cadence not found in Standard Hindi

### The signature sound: *ahai*

The most obvious marker is the **auxiliary verb *ahai* (अहै)**, which replaces Hindi's *hai* (है). Where a Hindi speaker says *yah sundar hai* (this is beautiful), an Awadhi speaker says **ee sundar ahai**. This single sound — the breathy, open *ahai* — is essentially Awadhi's acoustic fingerprint.

## Grammar that proves Awadhi is not "just Hindi with an accent"

### No ergative construction (fundamental structural difference)
Hindi uses the postposition *-ne* after the subject of transitive verbs in the past tense (*maine kiya* — "I did"). **Awadhi simply doesn't have this marker.** The doer and the acted-upon are grammatically more equal. This is not an accent variation — it is a fundamental structural difference.

### Different verb system
Awadhi retains true finite verb forms for present, past, and future (much like Bengali) rather than relying entirely on participles. Awadhi uses "हम करौं" (*ham karoṅ*, "I do") where Hindi says "मैं करूँ" (*main karūṅ*). Past tense frequently uses the suffix **-is**: *kha lihis* ("he/she ate"), *ban gais* ("it is made").

### Sanskrit-era grammatical forms preserved
- **Third-person pronouns:** *so/te* — recalling Sanskrit's *saḥ/te* — where Hindi uses *vah/ve*
- **Third-person genitive:** *tāsu* (his/hers) — preserving the ancient Sanskrit *-sya* ending, lost in Hindi
- **Reflexive pronoun:** *nij* (one's own) — carries philosophical weight about self-knowledge

### Different core grammatical particles
- **Possessives:** *mor* (my), *tor* (your) — warm and round where Hindi's *mera/tera* are sharp
- **Dative:** *ka* (not Hindi's *ko*)
- **Locative:** *ma* (not Hindi's *mein*)

### Gender flexibility
Where Hindi "rigorously maintains" grammatical gender, Awadhi is described as "a little loose yet largely preserved" — more flexible.

### Productive reduplication
*Jāt-jāt* means "going on and on." Partial reduplications like *hãpaṭ-dãpaṭ* (panting) create onomatopoeic effects unique to the language.

### Aspect-heavy verb system
Many ways to say "doing, used to do, kept on doing" give Awadhi storytelling a slow, cinematic pace. Elders recount events with strings of continuous and habitual forms that sound like a live movie.

## The script that was killed: Kaithi

Perhaps the most visually fascinating and historically tragic aspect of Awadhi's story is the erasure of its native script.

**What Kaithi was:**
- A highly cursive, rapid-flowing Brahmi-descended script used across North India
- Named after the **Kayastha caste** — traditional record-keepers, administrators, and scribes
- Derived from the ancient **Gupta script**
- Designed for speed — the preferred medium for legal documents, revenue records, title deeds, personal correspondence
- Used by **both Hindus and Muslims** — it was a neutral, secular script
- **Sher Shah Suri adopted Kaithi for his royal court in 1540**
- In the **1880s**, the British standardized it as the official script of judicial courts in Bihar
- **1854: 77,368 school primers in Kaithi versus only 25,151 in Devanagari** — Kaithi had 3× more textbooks

**How it was killed:**
- The printing press disadvantaged Kaithi — too cursive for standardized metal type fonts compared to blockier Devanagari
- The **Nagari Pracharini Sabha** (founded 1893) lobbied for Devanagari as the sole standard
- Muslim groups pushed Persian script; Kaithi was orphaned
- Indian nationalists and linguistic purists campaigned against Kaithi
- Its very neutrality — neither Hindu nor Muslim — became its weakness during communal script wars

**The consequences:**
- Today classified as a **"major extinct" script**
- Centuries of Awadhi administrative history, poetry, and personal diaries sit in archives, **entirely illegible** to modern Awadhi populations
- **Bihar courts today cannot read their own old land records** — creating real legal problems
- Encoded in **Unicode in 2009** — digital preservation of a script that barely has living readers

**Bhikhari Thakur**, the great Bhojpuri playwright, wrote all his works in Kaithi because "he had only learned the Kaithi alphabet and the Ramcharitmanas."

**The three scripts of Awadhi:**
1. **Devanagari** — dominant today
2. **Kaithi** — extinct but historically primary
3. **Perso-Arabic (Nastaliq)** — used in Muslim and courtly contexts, especially for Sufi poetry

# PART 5: THE LITERARY GIANTS

## Goswami Tulsidas (c.1532–1623): The Man Who Unlocked Sanskrit's Gate

The single most extraordinary fact about Tulsidas: he was a Sanskrit scholar of formidable ability who **chose** to write in Awadhi — and was nearly killed for it.

Orthodox Brahmins in Varanasi viewed his composition of the Ramcharitmanas in a "mere" vernacular as sacrilege. As scholar Asghar Wajahat framed it: *"Ramkatha ke upar Sanskrit ka tala laga hua thha"* — **"There was a lock of Sanskrit hanging on the story of Ram. Tulsidas broke that lock by writing in Awadhi."**

The Brahmin establishment "tried to humiliate Tulsidas while also making several attempts to kill him." According to legend, they tested his work by placing the manuscript at the bottom of a pile of Sanskrit scriptures in the Vishvanath temple. When the doors opened the next morning, the Ramcharitmanas was reportedly found at the top, bearing the inscription "Satyam Shivam Sundaram" with Shiva's signature. Whether true or apocryphal, the story crystallizes the revolution: **democratizing sacred knowledge through language choice.**

**The Ramcharitmanas:**
- Written 1574–75 in Awadhi doha-chaupai meter
- **12,800 lines across seven books**
- **108.7 million copies** printed by one press alone
- Present in virtually every Hindu household across North India
- Central to the Ramlila theatrical tradition (UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage)
- **UNESCO Memory of the World Asia-Pacific Register (May 2024)**
- An 18th-century **Arabic translation** demonstrates the text traveled to West Asia and was read across the Islamic world

**The Hanuman Chalisa:**
- A forty-verse Awadhi hymn
- Recited daily by hundreds of millions worldwide — from corporate executives in Mumbai to diaspora communities in Trinidad
- Most are completely unaware the words leaving their lips are Awadhi

**Other works:** *Parvati Mangal*, *Janaki Mangal*, and others — all in Awadhi

Tulsidas also founded the **Ramlila theatrical tradition**, further institutionalized by his student **Megha Bhagat in 1625**.

Historian Vincent Smith called Tulsidas **"the greatest man of his age in India, greater even than Akbar himself."**

**The core irony: Over 600 million people revere a text composed in a language that the modern Indian state refuses to acknowledge as independent.**

## Malik Muhammad Jayasi (1477–1542): The Sufi Who Wrote a Hindu Epic

Jayasi was a practicing Muslim, a pir of the **Chishti Sufi order** from the town of **Jais in Amethi district**. He was blind in one eye, heavily pockmarked from smallpox, and by most accounts physically unassuming.

His *Padmavat* (1540) is perhaps the most remarkable act of cross-cultural literary creation in Indian history. He wrote the story of the Hindu Queen Padmavati and the Muslim emperor Alauddin Khalji — but as a **Sufi allegory**: Ratan Sen represents the human soul, Padmavati represents wisdom, Alauddin represents the illusion of *maya*, and Chittor represents the body.

The *Padmavat* "travelled far and wide, from Arakan (Myanmar) to the Deccan" and was eagerly copied in Persian, Bengali, and other languages. It inspired at least **12 adaptations** and, centuries later, Sanjay Leela Bhansali's 2018 Bollywood blockbuster *Padmaavat*.

Jayasi concluded his epic with a disarming honesty rare in Indian literary tradition: *"I have made up the story and related it."*

**The irony is layered: one of the greatest works of "Hindu" romantic literature was written by a Muslim mystic, in the language of Ram's birthplace, using Persian poetic forms. Awadhi was the meeting ground — the language where Hindu and Muslim literary traditions could merge without contradiction.**

## Kabir (c.1398–1518): The Radical in the People's Tongue

Kabir's connection to Awadhi is confirmed by linguistic analysis of his major work, the *Bijak*, which George Grierson classified as "written in old form of Awadhi language" in the Mirzapuri dialect. Kabir himself described his language as *"pancmel khichri"* — a hotch-potch of vernaculars — but Awadhi was the primary ingredient.

His **541 hymns** included in the Sikh *Guru Granth Sahib* (the highest non-Sikh contribution) ensured that Awadhi words entered the sacred literature of Sikhism as well. An old adage says Awadhi was to the North what Greek was to the Western tradition.

### Amir Khusrau and Awadhi's DNA in Hindi-Urdu

**Amir Khusrau (d. 1325)**, the "father of Hindustani poetry," composed in Awadhi. Modern Hindustani (the ancestor of both Hindi and Urdu) developed when Persian-speaking Khari Boli speakers from Delhi migrated to the Awadh region. The mixing of Khari Boli's "roughness" with Awadhi's "softness" created *Hindavi*, which became the foundation of Hindi-Urdu. **In a very real sense, Hindi carries Awadhi DNA in its bones.**

# PART 6: THE LIVING CULTURE

## Songs for every season and sorrow

Awadhi folk music maps onto the emotional calendar of rural life with remarkable precision.

### Major folk genres

**Sohar** — Birth songs sung by women to celebrate childbirth. Gentle melodies comparing the newborn to Lord Rama or Krishna, weaving blessings for the child's prosperity into devotional imagery. Full of teasing the new parents, playful references to gods and ancestors.

**Chaiti** — Spring songs of the month of Chaitra (March–April), celebrating new beginnings with lively rhythms. Often performed during Ramnavami. Example lyric: *"Chait māse janme Ram, ho Rāmā"* ("O Rama, you were born in the month of Chait").

**Kajri** — Monsoon songs (July–September), expressing both the joy of rain and the ache of romantic separation — the woman longing for her beloved during the season when the world is wet and green and impossibly alive. Uses looming clouds and rain as metaphors for separation, fertility, and desire. Example: *"Ras barse badariya sawan ki"* ("Clouds of the rain are pouring nectar"). The classical singer **Girija Devi** was among Kajri's greatest interpreters.

**Birha** — Literally *virah* (separation). Perhaps the most emotionally raw genre. Associated with the **Ahir** (pastoral) community. Expresses the pain of lovers and families divided by migration for work. In a region where millions have left for Delhi, Mumbai, and the Gulf states, Birha carries devastating contemporary resonance. Emotionally intense and improvisational.

**Aalha** — Heroic ballads recounting the 12th-century warriors Alha and Udal who fought 52 wars. Performed by duos in antiphonal singing style with dholak and nagada accompaniment. High, dramatic register during fairs and religious gatherings.

**Nirgun** — Philosophical songs about the formless divine and the transience of life, deeply influenced by Kabir.

**Gaari** — Playful, poetic wedding insults — a specific oral tradition being lost as linguistic context disappears.

### Work songs (the soundtrack of agricultural life)

| Genre | Context |
|-------|---------|
| Jatsar | Sung while grinding grain |
| Ropani | Sung while transplanting paddy |
| Kolhui Geet | Sung during oil-pressing |
| Palki Geet | Sung while carrying palanquins |
| Khalihan ke Geet | Sung on the threshing floor |

*The entire rhythm of agricultural life once had its own soundtrack — in Awadhi.*

**Modern torchbearer:** **Malini Awasthi** has achieved national acclaim for bringing Awadhi folk traditions out of villages and onto mainstream, global stages.

## Theater and performance

**Ramlila:**
- Founded by Tulsidas's student Megha Bhagat in 1625
- Performed in Awadhi during Navaratri across North India
- The **Ramnagar Ramlila** (Varanasi), patronized by the Kashi Naresh (King of Varanasi), spans **31 days**
- **UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage**
- "Brings the whole population together, without distinction of caste, religion, or age"
- Has spread to **Trinidad, Fiji, Mauritius, Guyana, and Suriname** through the Girmitiya diaspora

**Nautanki** — Traveling musical theater with exaggerated acting, topical jokes, and folk tales. Awadhi speech and song forms are recurrent. Evolved under Nawabi patronage, mixing Awadhi, Braj, Khari Boli, and Urdu.

**Swang** — Semi-improvised folk theater with parody, cross-dressing, and local satire.

## The vocabulary of the Dastarkhwan: Awadhi cuisine

Awadhi cuisine — known globally as "Lucknowi food" — carries a vocabulary that reveals an entire philosophy of eating. It synthesizes Middle Eastern, Central Asian, and Indian techniques with a Nawabi aesthetic that values **patience over heat**.

### Cooking techniques with specific Awadhi terminology

| Term | Meaning | Story |
|------|---------|-------|
| **Dum Pukht** | "Breathe in + cook" — slow-cooking in a sealed pot over low fire | Attributed to Nawab Asaf-ud-Daula around 1784. Ingredients sealed with dough, cooked gently in their own trapped vapors. |
| **Galawat** | The art of extreme tenderization — meat that melts on the tongue | Born from the legend of a **toothless Nawab** who demanded kebabs he could eat without teeth. Led to the **Galouti kebab** with its 150+ spice blend. |
| **Dhungar** | Quick smoking using live charcoal and clarified butter | A finishing technique for flavor |
| **Zamin-Doz** | Cooking fish or meat in a sealed pit dug into the earth | Ancient technique |

## Ganga-Jamuni Tehzeeb: the syncretic personality

Awadhi's cultural personality is defined by **tehzeeb** — refined civilization, courtesy, and grace. Lucknow is known as the "City of Adab" (city of courtesy). The **Ganga-Jamuni Tehzeeb** — the syncretic Hindu-Muslim culture of the Awadh region — manifests in:

- Kashmiri Muslim carpet makers featuring Durga
- Muslim sculptors crafting Hindu idols
- Hindu craftsmen building Muharram *tazias*
- Both communities participating in each other's festivals
- Awadhi serving as the language of this fusion

# PART 7: THE DISAPPEARING ACT

## The five forces killing Awadhi

### 1. The census classification weapon
By classifying 48 distinct languages as "mother tongues under Hindi" since 1971, the Census inflated Hindi's share from 30.39% to 43.63%. This has cascading policy consequences: no official speaker count → no basis for demanding educational/media rights → no recognition → no funding → no institutional infrastructure. The cycle is self-reinforcing.

Because of prestige bias, younger Awadhi speakers increasingly claim "Standard Hindi" as their mother tongue to census enumerators, falsely inflating Hindi's numbers while artificially deflating Awadhi's survival metrics.

### 2. Zero educational infrastructure
Standard Hindi is the medium of instruction in every school across the Awadh region. **No Awadhi-medium school exists in India.** The American India Foundation posed the question: "Why would it be easy for speakers of Awadhi to adopt a new language to attend school?"

Children arrive speaking Awadhi and are taught in a language that is related but grammatically distinct — no ergative marker, different pronouns, different verb conjugations, different auxiliary verbs. The educational system treats their mother tongue as invisible.

Nepal has an official Awadhi curriculum for classes 1–10, but community leader **Yuraj Gupta 'Bhairagi'** reports it "has yet to be properly implemented."

One person recalled: **"I spoke Awadhi until I entered the school gates, at which point I'd switch to Khari Boli."**

### 3. Stigma: the deepest wound
Awadhi writer **Priyanka Shukla** (2025): *"Growing up, I was shy — almost embarrassed — about speaking Awadhi. It didn't feel 'cool.' It felt rural. Crude. Backward. I remember consciously switching to Nepali or Hindi outside the home to blend in better."*

Bollywood reinforces this: Awadhi accents are used for "rustic or comedic figures" — the village simpleton, the comic relief — rather than dignified characters.

Britannica: *"Speaking standard Hindi gives as much status to people in this region as speaking English gives in the south of India. People in search of new jobs, marriages, and the like must use standard Hindi."*

Parents in cities deliberately correct children away from Awadhi, even at home, to ease school and job prospects. As one activist put it: *"The real issue is people no longer take pride in Awadhi. Parents decide school language, children lose it."*

### 4. Media vacuum
There is no Awadhi television channel. No Awadhi newspaper of note. No dedicated Awadhi radio station in India. The Hindi media landscape is so saturated in the Awadh region that Awadhi has no commercial media presence whatsoever.

### 5. Urbanization and generational shift
Even in Lucknow and Ayodhya — the historic capitals of Awadh — Standard Hindi and Urdu dominate public life. Awadhi retreats to private, familial, and rural domains.

The generational shift: while grandparents converse exclusively in Awadhi, and parents maintain functional bilingualism, the youth primarily possess only passive understanding. They comprehend the language when spoken by elders but are unable, or entirely unwilling, to speak it themselves.

**86% of Western UP's youth use only Hindi.**

### UNESCO status and the data vacuum
- **UNESCO World Atlas of Languages:** "Potentially Vulnerable"
- **Ethnologue:** Level 5 (Developing), "Stable" — but concedes "direct evidence is lacking"
- The discrepancy between these two classifications is itself telling

**No formal intergenerational transmission study has ever been conducted for Awadhi in India.** No comprehensive language attitudes survey exists. No systematic documentation of domain loss has been undertaken.

For comparison: **Welsh (880,000 speakers) has been the subject of hundreds of sociolinguistic studies, annual government surveys, and continuous monitoring.** Awadhi (38 million speakers) has zero.

**The very absence of data is a form of neglect.**

### The parallel that should terrify
Awadhi's trajectory parallels **Braj Bhasha** (once the literary language of Krishna devotion, now reduced to rural speech in the Mathura region). **Maithili** broke this pattern by securing Eighth Schedule recognition in 2003 — the model Awadhi advocates seek to replicate.

> **"Awadhi is endangered enough to worry elders, but invisible enough that even UNESCO doesn't name it."**

# PART 8: THE FIGHT TO SAVE IT

## Political recognition: promises and paralysis

Awadhi is among **38–39 languages** demanding Eighth Schedule inclusion.

**Key political timeline:**
- **2003:** Sitakant Mohapatra Committee formed to develop criteria for new Eighth Schedule languages. No actionable results.
- **2012:** Inter-Ministerial Committee constituted. No actionable results.
- **2017:** Writ petition seeking Awadhi + Bhojpuri Eighth Schedule inclusion. Referred to committees.
- **August 2021:** Minister of State for Home Nityanand Rai told the Rajya Sabha: *"No fixed criteria"* and *"no time-frame can be fixed"* for considering Awadhi's inclusion.
- **February 18, 2025:** UP Chief Minister Yogi Adityanath announced **Awadhi, Bhojpuri, Braj, and Bundeli would be incorporated into UP Assembly proceedings with live translation** — making UP the first state assembly with multilingual translation. Also promised separate language academies.
- **Status (late 2025):** Only the Surdas Braj Bhasha Academy has been built (Govardhan, Mathura) — not yet operational. **The Awadhi academy has not materialized.**

## Digital lifelines

| Initiative | Details | Impact |
|------------|---------|--------|
| **Awadhi Wikipedia** | Launched May 20, 2020 after years of Wikimedia Incubator advocacy. By March 2021: 2,432 articles, 988 registered users, 2 active editors/month. | Infrastructure exists but severely undermanned for a 38M-speaker language |
| **Google Translate** | Added Awadhi in 2024 as part of 110-language expansion using PaLM 2 AI model. | First time Awadhi was accessible through a major digital translation platform. *"A language with 38 million speakers waited for AI to notice it."* |
| **STAGE OTT platform** | Appeared on Shark Tank India, raised $12.5M Series B. 20M users with Haryanvi/Rajasthani content. Plans to expand into Awadhi, Maithili, Magahi. | Commercial interest in regional content |
| **UP Film Policy 2023** | 50% production subsidy for Awadhi films (up to ₹2 crore) vs. 25% for Hindi/English | Meaningful incentive, but Awadhi film industry barely exists to use it |
| **YouTube** | Most organic vector for Awadhi revival. Folk music, cultural content, comedy growing. | "YouTubers have now started speaking in Awadhi" — creating a "parallel industry" of dialect media |

## Grassroots efforts

- **"I love languages"** YouTube channel — Awadhi grammar tutorials for global audiences
- **"The Treasured Word with Tara Linhardt"** podcast — documents native speakers, Nepal regional variations
- **Siya Art & Craft** (Nepal NGO) — teaches Awadhi arts and language in villages
- **"Jhankar of Awadhi"** theatrical troupe — tours schools in UP
- **Caribbean Hindustani Inc.** (led by Visham Bhimull) — preservation across Trinidad, Guyana, Suriname
- **Sangraksha** — digital humanities project for eastern India rural cultural histories
- **Lucknow University and BHU** — sponsor seminars and courses on Awadhi culture
- A handful of Awadhi magazines and radio shows exist (limited by funding)

## What's working and what's not

**Working:** YouTube/social media (organic grassroots revival), Nepal's census growth and official recognition, UP Assembly translation (symbolic but unprecedented), film subsidies, Google Translate, Awadhi Wikipedia infrastructure.

**Not working:** Eighth Schedule inclusion (decades of demand, zero progress), language academies (promised repeatedly, never built), education (zero Awadhi-medium instruction in India), documentation (no comprehensive modern corpus, no NLP tools beyond Google Translate).

**The fundamental structural problem:** Unlike Welsh (Welsh Language Board), Māori (Te Taura Whiri), or Hebrew (Academy of the Hebrew Language), **Awadhi has no dedicated institutional anchor responsible for its survival.** The very state that should protect it actively subsumes it under Hindi.

## The Ayodhya paradox

The Ram Mandir construction was accompanied by a reported **$10 billion transformation plan** for Ayodhya, which received massive visitor numbers in 2024 (reports range from 16.45 million to 135.5 million — figures conflict across sources). The word "Awadh" derives from "Ayodhya." The Ramcharitmanas that made Ram worship central to Hindu devotion was written in Awadhi.

The state government has promised to promote "Awadhi delicacies" and crafts during festivals. An April 2024 tourism plan even recommended teaching basic Awadhi phrases to guides. **Billions flow to the city that gave the language its name, but the language itself receives no government funding, no constitutional recognition, and no institutional support.**

## Bollywood's Awadhi secret

### India's most beloved "Hindi" family film is actually an Awadhi remake
*Nadiya Ke Paar* (1982) was filmed entirely in Awadhi. The same director remade it in Hindi as *Hum Aapke Hain Koun..!* (1994) — the highest-grossing Hindi film of its era. India's most beloved "Hindi" family film is, at its origin, an Awadhi story.

### Gabbar Singh speaks Awadhi
The most quoted villain in Indian cinema history — Gabbar Singh from *Sholay* (1975) — speaks a mix of Khari Boli and Awadhi. The dialect was inspired by Dilip Kumar's character in *Gunga Jumna* (1961), which used Awadhi dialect extensively and became the blueprint for the "Angry Young Man" era of 1970s Bollywood. Every Indian quoting Gabbar is channeling Awadhi without knowing it.

### Hundreds of millions chant Awadhi daily — and don't know it
The Hanuman Chalisa, recited daily by hundreds of millions of Hindus, is in Awadhi. This makes Awadhi the most-recited non-Sanskrit sacred language in Hinduism.

### Official in Fiji, invisible in India
Fiji Hindi has been an official language of Fiji since 1997. In India, where the language originated, it has zero official status.

### Hindi-Urdu has Awadhi DNA
Amir Khusrau (d. 1325), the father of Hindustani poetry, composed in Awadhi. The mixing of Khari Boli with Awadhi created Hindavi → Hindi-Urdu.

### Awadhi almost became "Hindi"
Before the 19th century, Awadhi and Braj Bhasha were the prestige literary languages. Khari Boli was considered rough and unsophisticated. Had Fort William College chosen Awadhi, the linguistic identity of 500+ million people would be different.

### A Padma Shri for writing in a language the government doesn't recognize
In 2022, Dr. Vidya Vindu Singh received the Padma Shri. The government simultaneously honors excellence in Awadhi and refuses to acknowledge Awadhi as a language.

### The script that dominated North India — and vanished
In 1854, Kaithi had 3× more school textbooks than Devanagari. Today it's effectively dead. Bihar courts can't read their own land records.

### Google Translate waited until 2024
38 million speakers, one of the world's greatest literary traditions. Not on Google Translate until AI made it computationally convenient.

### An Awadhi epic was written in a British prison
Dwarka Prasad Mishra wrote *Krishnayan* (1942) while imprisoned. Freedom fighters sang Awadhi *chaupais* as acts of cultural resistance.

### The Ramcharitmanas was translated into Arabic
An 18th-century Arabic translation demonstrates the Awadhi text traveled to West Asia and was read across the Islamic world.

### The $10 billion Ayodhya paradox
Billions flow to the city that gave the language its name. The language receives ₹0 in government support.

### The "Pehle Aap" connection
Lucknow's famed extreme politeness — the legendary "pehele āp" (after you) — traces directly to Awadhi etiquette and its culture of *tehzeeb*.

### Surprising admirers
- **Chris Shackle** (British linguist) — published modern Awadhi poetry in English
- **Abida Parveen** — sang an Awadhi Sufi bhajan on YouTube
- "Awadh Kumar" — a Bhojpuri comic satirizing language pride that went viral in 2025

# PART 11: VERIFICATION NOTES AND DATA CONFLICTS

## Honest flags for the website builder

### Data conflicts requiring verification

1. **Ayodhya visitors 2024:** One source says 16.45 million, another says 135.5 million. These may refer to different time periods (post-temple opening rush vs. full year) or different measurement methodologies. Verify before using.

2. **Awadhi Wikipedia status:** Report 2 describes "an active Awadhi Wikipedia." Report 3 says it's "only in incubator with few hundred words." Report 4 provides the definitive answer: launched May 20, 2020, with 2,432 articles by March 2021. **Use Report 4's data.**

3. **MLK Jr. inspired by Ramcharitmanas:** Claimed in Report 3 without a source. This is **likely apocryphal** and should not be used on the site without rigorous sourcing.

4. **Speaker estimates vary wildly:** 3.85M (census) to 80M (political claim). The most defensible estimate, used by multiple independent linguistic sources, is **~38 million**. Use this as the primary figure, with census figure and range shown for context.

5. **"Awadhi not taught in any institution":** This is a widely cited claim, but Lucknow University and BHU do offer Awadhi-related courses (within Hindi/linguistics departments). No **dedicated** Awadhi department or Awadhi-medium instruction exists. Be precise.

6. **Golden age dates:** Reports vary between 14th–17th and 15th–18th century. The literary evidence supports **1379 (Chandayan) to 1623 (death of Tulsidas)** as the peak, with Nawabi patronage extending influence through the 18th century.

### Claims that are strong and multiply sourced
- Census figure of 3.85M (official government data)
- 38M independent estimate (multiple sources)
- Different Prakrit ancestors (Saksena, 1938)
- No ergative construction (structural difference)
- Kaithi script 3× more textbooks than Devanagari (1854)
- 1971 expansion from 10 to 48 languages under Hindi
- Nepal 72% growth (census data)
- Fiji Hindi official status since 1997
- Ramcharitmanas UNESCO inscription (May 2024)
- Google Translate addition (2024)

### Claims with single or weak sourcing
- 108.7 million Ramcharitmanas copies "by one press alone" (single source)
- 80 million political claim (attributed to unnamed politicians)
- "86% of Western UP youth use only Hindi" (single survey reference)
- $10 billion Ayodhya transformation plan (media reports, possibly inflated)
- Vincent Smith's quote about Tulsidas being "greater than Akbar" (historical, unverifiable in modern terms)

### Genuine gaps where data simply doesn't exist
These are documented throughout and are **part of the story itself**:
- No time-series for Awadhi speakers in India (census hides them)
- No literacy rate for Awadhi specifically
- No count of Awadhi publications
- No language attitude studies
- No intergenerational transmission data
- No social media analytics
- No comprehensive corpus

**The absence of this data is not a research failure. It is the most powerful evidence of systemic neglect. A language with 38 million speakers should not have blank cells where data belongs. Use the blanks as design elements.**
